Current projects & interests
Framework
AI Use with Integrity

In the spring of 2025 I noticed a repeated complaint from students: teachers said "don't use AI" but clearly were using AI themselves. At the same time, I kept hearing a different complaint from teachers: students didn't understand that teachers and students write for fundamentally different reasons, so of course their use of AI would be different. But no one seemed able to explain exactly where the line was between outsourcing necessary experiences and simply outsourcing drudgery. Many teenagers would describe their homework as drudgery; many teachers would describe that same homework as necessary practice.

The thing is, none of this problem is new. Students, teachers, parents, coaches, and institutions have always relied on outside help, some of it generative. AI simply accelerated the speed, scale, and accessibility of generative assistance, forcing old questions into unusual prominence. Those questions include issues at the intersection of ownership, responsibility, and identity.

Since hearing those original complaints, I've been developing a framework for thinking about-and perhaps more importantly discussing—generative assistance, delegated cognitive work, and authenticity. The framework is an attempt to create a shared language of cognitive labor, not just rules. It is currently being revised based on educator feedback gathered during a June 2026 AI workshop.

Ethics
Ethical Computer Science

With the advent of extremely powerful large language models, students can increasingly build tools that reach far beyond their immediate community. A few solid ideas and enough persistence can now produce software that would have required a team of developers only a few years ago. But, these same students are still developing their executive functioning, and have barely begun to understand what their own view of ethics and morality being a "good human" might entail. But it's not because they can't develop that sense. It's because they haven't been guided and challenged enough in that lane, and because they view technical spaces as ones in which tools are neutral and functional programs are good programs. In a world where adolescents are building technology that you might rely on next year, we should want them to be thinking and reflecting in intentional, structured ways about what a good program is.

That "goodness" extends beyond functional and into morally good, defensible, unbiased. Students should consider responsibilty, impact, security, privacy, and even the concept of what it means to flourish. But training that sense requires ethics and moral questioning throughout their technical training, not just as an add-on before they sign off on shipping code. This is the work of embedding ethics in our computer science education.

Co-Curriculars
Robotics Team

As advisor to the school's FTC robotics team, I see a side of students that they may not show in classes, sports, or in the dorm. Many of my most devoted students on the competition team are truly engaging with it as a team. Students learn about leading, working towards a common goal, sharing accountability, and managing projects outside the confinement of classes, grades, and the push towards the college transcript.

For the most dedicated students, it will be one of the most enduring commitments they make in high school, and one in which they have a chance to make a true impact and leave a legacy. Students design, build, program, and compete with a robot of their own making, on a with its own structure and responsibility. This is one of the most "real life" things they can do, and they know it. In this space, responsibility, passion, and academic inquiry become real, and that reality is both rare and transformative.

Pedagogy
Engineering as Liberal Arts

In my time working with engineers in graduate school and industry, I noticed that the best engineers are those who engaged with a liberal arts eduction. These engineers have encountered and engaged with a wide range of ideas and are equipped to speak with a wide range of people about their work, adjusting their arguments to match their audience. On the flipside of that experience in industry is my experience in education: engineering is often viewed with suspicion in the best liberal arts institutions.

That said, the best engineering classes often teach the true span of liberal arts: science and mathematics through calculations of scale, angles of construction, and physical mechanics, arts through design and aesthetics, and humanities through research and technical writing, oral presentation skills, and reasoning. As a result, these classes are uniquely well-suited to be a microcosm of the liberal arts education as well as a place to connect cross-disciplinary learning and test out ideas. I'm interested in the ways in which engineering is an unexpected entry point to the broader liberal arts.

Courses I teach
CSC·401
Programming in Python
Introductory · T1, T2
contributor
An introduction to computational thinking primarily through the exploration of the Python programming language. Students design and code their own programming projects throughout the course. The course sometimes includes additional applications such as programming introductory data analysis, game development, and vibe coding.
CSC·411
Robotics
Introductory · T1, T3
author · stable
A hands-on, project-based course where students use the engineering process to engage in science-driven decision making and review physical concepts involved in the design and construction of a robot. Students create robots capable of complex behaviors while exploring different aspects of robot design and how they come together in the creation of a robot, including ethical and cultural elements surrounding the use of artificial intelligence.
CSC·454
Computer Organization & Hardware
Intermediate · T3
author · building
An introduction to the organization and design of computer systems. Students invent and explore the essential hardware and software components of computational devices. Topics include digital logic, circuit design, memory architectures and hierarchies, and assembly language(s). Over the course of the term, students will complete a series of projects that gradually build the components of a simple computer system in simulation.
CSC·551
Data Structures & Algorithms
Advanced · T3
author · in revision
An opportunity for students to study, build, test, and apply data structures such as stacks, queues, linked lists, binary trees, heaps, hash maps and graphs, including formulating appropriate algorithms to support their data. Along the way, students develop software engineering skills potentially including collaborative coding, source control, and test-driven development.
CSC·571
Autonomous Systems
Advanced · T2
author · in revision
A hands-on, project-driven introduction to the design and control of autonomous systems. Students plan and build cyber-physical and autonomous systems in self-selected domains. Their projects drive explorations of necessary robotics, artificial intelligence, and control engineering to achieve their goals. Woven throughout this work, the students explore the ethical and societal implications of these systems.

About

Katelin Lewellen is an educator, programmer, and designer who teaches and plays at the intersection of computer science, engineering, and mathematics. She has taught at every level from middle school to graduate school; in public schools, private schools, university, and community college. She currently teaches at Phillips Academy Andover. In the classroom, Katelin challenges students to develop their own mental models and processes for thinking, designing, and problem-solving. Her classes emphasize hands-on exploration where students make choices defensible by both justification and reflection.

Katelin is committed to intertwining computer science with ethics, problem solving, human relationships, research, and judgement. These interests are reflected in her emphasis on both written and oral argumentation. She is focused on cultivating interesting learning environments seeded with thorny problems for students to grapple with. She has spent time developing a framework for helping students and teachers discuss generative assistance, AI, and where cognitive work belongs. Katelin looks at AI with a cautiously optimistic view, but remains deeply concerned with preserving human judgment, responsibility, and authenticity. She coaches the school's FIRST Tech Challenge robotics team (PARC 28170), and enjoys casual creation on campus with tools both physical and digital.

Wherever you meet her, Katelin is a detail-loving systems thinker who finds joy in both the messy middle of making and the clean precision of intentional design. She is committed to the never-ending task of making classes as sustainable and joyful as they are rigorous, for both students and teachers. She is also one of the most analog computer science teachers you are likely to meet.

You can ask Katelin about knitting, quilting, baking, running, baseball, or books and puzzles of all sorts. She lives in Andover with her husband, three kids, and a large dog.
