Current projects & interests
Pedagogy
Engineering as a Back Door to the Liberal Arts
Building a robot is an act of writing, reasoning, and design — it just doesn't look like a humanities class. I'm interested in how engineering and making can serve as unexpected entry points to the broader habits of mind that a liberal arts education is actually after.
Framework
AI Use with Integrity
A structured classroom model for navigating AI — not a list of what's allowed, but a shared vocabulary for when AI helps, when it doesn't, and how to stay in the driver's seat. Built around five use categories that keep students responsible for their own thinking.
Ethics
Ethical Computer Science
CS taught as a discipline with civic weight. Students don't just build things — they examine who benefits, who's harmed, and what assumptions are baked into the systems they create. Ethics isn't a unit at the end of the course; it runs through everything.
Competition
FIRST Tech Challenge
Advisor to the school's FTC robotics team. Students design, build, program, and compete with a robot of their own making — combining hardware, software, and strategy under real competition pressure, on a real deadline.
Courses I teach
CSC·401
Programming in Python
Introductory · T1, T2
contributor
An introduction to computational thinking primarily through the exploration of the Python programming language. Students design and code their own programming projects throughout the course. The course sometimes includes additional applications such as programming introductory data analysis, game development, and vibe coding.
CSC·411
Robotics
Introductory · T1, T3
author · stable
A hands-on, project-based course where students use the engineering process to engage in science-driven decision making and review physical concepts involved in the design and construction of a robot. Students create robotics capable of complex behaviors while exploring different aspects of robot design and how they come together in the creation of a robot, including ethical and cultural elements surrounding the use of artificial intelligence.
CSC·454
Computer Organization & Hardware
Intermediate · T3
author · building
An introduction to the organization and design of computer systems. Students invent and explore the essential hardware and software commponents of computational devices. Topics include digital logic, circuit design, memory architectures and hierarchies, and assembly language(s). Over the course of the term, students will complete a series of projects that gradually build the components of a simple computer system in simulation.
CSC·551
Data Structures & Algorithms
Advanced · T3
author · in revision
An opportunity for students to study, build, test, and apply data structures such as stacks, queues, linked lists, binary trees, heaps, hash maps and graphs, including formulating appropriate algorithms to support their data. Along the way, students develop software engineering skills potentially including collaborative coding, source control, and test-driven development.
CSC·571
Autonomous Systems
Advanced · T2
author · in revision
A hands-on, project-driven introduction to the design and control of autonomous systems. Students plan and build cyber-physical and autonomous systems in self-selected domains. Their projects drive explorations of necessary robotics, artificial intelligence, and control engineering to achieve their goals. Woven throughout this work, the students explore the ethical and societal implications of these systems.
MTH

Mathematics
Various math classes, most recently precalculus and calculus, taught alongside other Andover faculty. I teach math with a focus on exploration, reasoning, and connection which draws from traditions of complex instruction, problem-based learning, and standards-based grading.

About

Katelin Lewellen is an educator, programmer, and designer who teaches and plays at the intersection of computer science, engineering, and mathematics. She has taught at every level from middle school to graduate school; in public middle school, private high school, university, and community college. She currently teaches at Phillips Academy Andover. In the classroom, Katelin challenges students to develop their own mental models and processes: for thinking, designing, and problem-solving. Her classes emphasize hands-on exploration where students make choices backed by both justification and reflection.

Katelin is committed to intertwining computer science with ethics, problem solving, human relationships, writing, research, and oral argumentation. She is particularly interested in cultivating interesting learning environments with intractable problems for students to grapple with. She has spent time developing a classroom framework for student AI use, but remains deeply suspicious about AI's role in a society that values being both human and humane. She coaches the school's FIRST Tech Challenge robotics team (PARC 28170), and enjoys casual creation on campus with tools both physical and digital.

Wherever you meet her, Katelin is a detail-loving systems thinker who finds joy in both the messy middle of making and the clean precision of intentional design. She is committed to the sisyphean task of making complex work sustainable and joyful for both students and teachers, and remains one of the most analog computer science teachers you are likely to meet. You can ask her about knitting, quilting, baking, running, baseball, or books and puzzles of all sorts. She lives in Andover with her husband, three kids, and a large dog.
