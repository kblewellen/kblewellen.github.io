Current projects & interests
Pedagogy
Engineering as a Back Door to the Liberal Arts
Building a robot is an act of writing, reasoning, and design — it just doesn't look like a humanities class. I'm interested in how engineering and making can serve as unexpected entry points to the broader habits of mind that a liberal arts education is actually after.
Framework
AI Use with Integrity
A structured classroom model for navigating AI — not a list of what's allowed, but a shared vocabulary for when AI helps, when it doesn't, and how to stay in the driver's seat. Built around five use categories that keep students responsible for their own thinking.
Ethics
Ethical Computer Science
CS taught as a discipline with civic weight. Students don't just build things — they examine who benefits, who's harmed, and what assumptions are baked into the systems they create. Ethics isn't a unit at the end of the course; it runs through everything.
Competition
FIRST Tech Challenge
Advisor to the school's FTC robotics team. Students design, build, program, and compete with a robot of their own making — combining hardware, software, and strategy under real competition pressure, on a real deadline.
Courses I teach
CSC·401
Programming in Python
Introductory · T1, T2
contributor
An introduction to computational thinking primarily through the exploration of the Python programming language. Students design and code their own programming projects throughout the course. The course sometines includes additional applications such as programming introductory data analysis, game development, and vibe coding.
CSC·411
Robotics
Introductory · T1, T3
author · stable
A hands-on, project-based course where students use the engineering process to engage in science-driven decision making and review physical concepts involved in the design and construction of a robot. Students create robotcs capable of complex behaviors while exploring different aspects of robot design and how they come together in the creation of a robot, including ethical and cultural elements surrounding the use of artificial intelligence.
CSC·454
Computer Organization & Hardware
Intermediate · T3
author · building
An introduction to the organization and design of computer systems. Students invent and explore the essential hardware and software commponents of computational devices. Topics include digital logic, circuit design, memory architectures and hierarchies, and assembly language(s). Over the course of the term, students will complete a series of projects that gradually build the components of a simple computer system in simulation.
CSC·551
Data Structures & Algorithms
Advanced · T3
author · in revision
An opportunity for students to study, build, test, and apply data structures such as stacks, queues, linked lists, binary trees, heaps, hash maps and graphs, including formulating appropriate algorithms to support their data. Along the way, students develop software engineering skills potentially including collaborative coding, source control, and test-driven development.
CSC·571
Autonomous Systems
Advanced · T2
author · in revision
A hands-on, project-driven introduction to the design and control of autonomous systems. Students plan and build cyber-physical and autonomous systems in self-selected domains. Their projects drive explorations of necesssy robotics, artificial intelligence, and control engineering to achieve their goals. Woven throughout this work, the students explore the ethical and societal implications of these systems.
MTH

Various math classes, most recently precalculus and calculus, taught alongside other Andover faculty. I teach math with a focus on exploration, reasoning, and connection which draws from traditions of complex instruction, problem-based learning, and standards-based grading.

About

I'm an educator, programmer, and designer working at the intersection of computer science, engineering, and mathematics education. I've taught at every level from middle school to graduate school — in public schools, private high schools, large universities, and community colleges — and I currently live and teach at Phillips Academy Andover. In the classroom, I challenge students to develop their own distinct way of thinking, designing, and problem-solving. My classes emphasize hands-on exploration, design, and reflection, centered on interesting environments with intractable problems to grapple with.

I'm committed to intertwining computer science with ethics, problem-solving, human relationships, writing, research, and oral argumentation. I've spent time developing a classroom framework for student AI use — but remain deeply suspicious about AI's role in a future society that stays fundamentally human and humane. I coach the school's FIRST Tech Challenge robotics team, and enjoy playing around with tools both physical (3D printer) and digital (vibe coding!).

Beyond the classroom, I'm a detail-loving systems thinker who finds equal joy in the messy process of making and the clean order of a well-built structure. I'm committed to the Sisyphean task of making complex work sustainable and joyful — and I remain, proudly, one of the most analog computer science teachers you are likely to meet.
