Building as a Way of Thinking

AI has dramatically lowered the cost of building educational software. The bottleneck is no longer technical implementation. The bottleneck is deciding what is worth building.

That framing turns out to be a pedagogical question. All the projects I worked on this week circled back to the same one: when generative resources are abundant — AI, but also a classmate, a tutor, Stack Overflow, or a teacher — how do students and teachers decide where the cognitive work should happen?

Students need help and teachers want learning. The difficult part is figuring out which work belongs to the learner and which can legitimately be handed off. The tools I built this week are one attempt to make that question more tractable. The framework underneath them is an attempt to make it discussable.

The visible outcome of the week is a set of prototypes. The deeper outcome is a research agenda I didn't know I was building toward.

Priors · Prototypes · Insights · Changes
Before the Workshop

The 5Cs model didn't start here. I built it in the fall of 2025, in response to a student complaint that I thought was completely fair: teachers were telling students not to use AI while visibly using it themselves. I didn't want to be part of that problem. I use AI extensively — at work and at home — but I try to be intentional about it. The framework is an attempt to encode those instincts into something I could share transparently with students, and that they could hold me to in return. In a workshop setting it serves a different purpose: a shared vocabulary for educators examining where their own instincts about AI actually land.

Workshop & Classroom
AI Framework: The 5Cs Model

The five Cs — Creativity, Core Skills, Clarify, Correct, Construct — classify AI use by cognitive role rather than permission status. Each C carries parallel guidance for students and teachers, plus CS Skills checkpoints that tie AI use to observable learning behaviors. It's the spine of every other tool in this suite.

What I Built

During the workshop I used AI-assisted development to build and iterate on tools that put the framework to work — in the classroom and the professional development room. Each tool below is labeled by its primary audience.

Workshop
AI Framework Survey

This pairs with Codes Against Academy — a pre-existing card deck in which educators (or students) rank AI use scenarios on a single dimension: how ethical or legal is this? It's a useful provocation, but it collapses a lot of complexity into one axis.

The survey adds a second one. Participants don't just rate the outcome — they identify the process, naming which of the 5Cs best describes how the AI was used. That creates a two-dimensional matrix: outcome (ethical/legal) on one axis, process (the relevant C) on the other. The result starts to map the space more precisely than "it depends," which is where most AI ethics conversations stall out.

The survey is also early validation. Consensus isn't the goal — when educators consistently disagree on a scenario, that's useful information: either the framework needs refinement, or the scenario has surfaced a genuine tension worth sitting with. So far the disagreements have been at least as interesting as the agreements.

Workshop & Classroom
Framework Writeup

The 5Cs model has a long-form home at ai_framework.html — student- and teacher-facing guidance for each C, plus the CS Skills checkpoints. This is the page students and colleagues are pointed to; the tools below are built on top of it.

Workshop & Classroom
Landing Page

The tools are hosted together on a password-protected portal at this site. The landing page organizes them for browsing and gives workshop participants and students a single place to find everything. Building and hosting the site was itself part of this week's work.

Student
411 Help

The most common failure mode when students use AI isn't dishonesty — it's vagueness. 411 Help walks students through a structured sequence before they interact with AI: which CS Skills are they working with, where exactly are they stuck, and what kind of help do they actually need. Each step is gated so students can't skip the self-assessment, and the skills selection doubles as a logbook entry tied to the 5Cs framework.

Teacher
AI Use Dashboard

A teacher-facing visualization for student AI logbooks. The dashboard shifts the question from "Did students use AI?" to "How are students using AI, and what patterns emerge over time?" Drop in logbook files and see a heatmap of AI help frequency broken down by student role and day — useful for spotting the day a cohort spiked in asks, or noticing a role that never asks at all, which is its own signal.

Student
VEX Test

Students write Python control programs before they touch the physical robot — but without a way to run the code, logic errors in motor timing or sensor branching only surface on competition day. Upload a .py file written for VEXcode and the app executes it in-browser, animating the robot's path on a scaled virtual field. I've been deliberately calling it "an imperfect simulator" — it's a debugging and visualization tool, not a certification rig, and I don't want students to mistake it for one.

Student
Gate Crashers

GlitchBot has scrambled the circuits in a digital fortress — students debug broken logic gate circuits to restore order before the chaos spreads. Built for Computer Organization (CSC 454), where students can memorize truth tables without ever developing intuition for how gates compose. The game format gives debugging a stakes structure and a completion signal that a worksheet can't replicate. Two levels live, more in progress.

What Building Revealed

The thing I kept running into: AI can produce almost any tool you describe to it. So the interesting question isn't "can I build this?" — it's "should I, and why?" The tools I'm proudest of from this week aren't the most technically ambitious. They're the ones that make student thinking more visible — that put the cognitive work back in front of the learner rather than hiding it inside an output.

That turned out to be a more durable criterion than I expected.

The Communication Problem

Most arguments I've heard about AI in schools aren't really about AI — they're about classification.

Students and teachers often agree that learning matters. What they don't have is a shared language for describing what happened.

A student might say:

I only used AI for help.

A teacher might say:

The student relied too heavily on AI.

Both can be true at the same time. The disagreement usually isn't about values — it's about categories.

"AI use" covers a wide range of things: brainstorming, clarification, construction, revision, substitution. Without more precise language, productive conversations are hard to have. The 5Cs framework is an attempt to supply that language.

The Framework

The 5Cs classify AI use by cognitive role, not by permission status.

The question it's asking isn't: "is this allowed?" But rather: "where is the cognitive work happening?"

  • What work is the learner doing?
  • What work is the AI doing?
  • Is that the right split for the learning goal?

This is especially pointed in computer science, where a student can produce a working program without having understood very much of it. Visible outputs obscure the learning process underneath them. A shared vocabulary for describing that process is one way to make it harder to hide.

The framework is a shared language, not a rulebook.

What Changed This Week

The workshop didn't just generate new tools. It changed how I think about the framework itself.

1. The Problem Is Bigger Than AI

I arrived thinking of the 5Cs as a framework for AI use. I'm leaving thinking of it as a framework for generative assistance more broadly. AI makes the issue visible, but many of the same questions arise when students work with tutors, classmates, parents, teachers, or online resources.

2. Outcome and Process Are Different Questions

One insight from the survey work was that people often evaluate outcomes and processes separately. A good outcome can be achieved through a problematic process, and a reasonable process can be directed toward a problematic outcome. The framework may need to make this distinction more explicit.

3. Role Matters

Many disagreements turned out not to be disagreements about AI at all. They were disagreements about the responsibilities of students, teachers, mentors, and institutions. The same use of generative assistance can feel appropriate in one role and inappropriate in another.

4. Human Contribution Matters

Some scenarios generated strong reactions that couldn't be explained by learning goals alone. Participants often objected when generative tools appeared to replace the uniquely human contribution expected of a role: a teacher's judgment, a student's understanding, or a person's authentic expression. This suggests that some disagreements about AI are really disagreements about what humans owe one another and the underlying expectation of authenticity.

5. The Real Question Changed

I originally thought the framework was helping people answer:

Is this AI use acceptable?

I now think it is helping them answer:

Am I outsourcing work that should still belong to me?
Open Questions
  • Are the categories sufficiently distinct?
  • Which scenarios create the greatest disagreement?
  • How do students classify their own AI use?
  • Do teachers classify the same scenarios differently?
  • How should the framework account for expertise level?
  • Which forms of generative assistance fall outside the current model?
Looking Forward

The most important outcome of this week isn't a tool or an artifact. It's a research question I didn't know I was building toward.

Can a shared framework actually help students and teachers develop a common language for generative assistance — and for the distribution of cognitive work?

The next phase of this work involves:

  • refining the written framework,
  • collecting educator and student classification data,
  • investigating areas of disagreement,
  • connecting to existing research on metacognition and CS education,
  • and exploring whether a framework-based approach can genuinely improve communication around AI use in schools.

The projects built this week are prototypes. The larger goal is not deciding whether AI is good or bad. It is giving students, teachers, and schools a shared language for discussing generative assistance so that AI can become one educational consideration among many, rather than the thing every conversation revolves around.
